Heuristics and Cognitive Bias: B2/C1 Lesson

B2/C1 ESL Connect Video lesson based on the tapescript: decisions, heuristics, representativeness bias, belief protection, disagreement and open-minded thinking.

Video link: Open on YouTube

Lesson Aim

Students watch a video about heuristics and cognitive biases, then discuss how quick mental shortcuts can help us make decisions but also narrow our thinking and affect difficult conversations.

Video structure: Part 1 explains everyday decisions, heuristics and shortcuts. Part 2 explores representativeness bias, perceived danger and why challenges to our beliefs can feel threatening.

B2/C1 Grammar: General Truths, Conditionals and Cautious Explanation

This video explains how our brains usually work, so the grammar focus is on zero and second conditionals, cause-and-effect language and cautious generalisations.

Example: If we had to consciously think through every decision, we would be overwhelmed.

Grammar Section 1: Zero Conditional for General Truths

1. Complete: “When heuristics cause poor choices, we ___ them cognitive biases.”

2. Which sentence expresses a general truth?

Grammar Section 2: Hypothetical Conditionals

1. Complete: “If we had to think through every decision, we ___ overwhelmed.”

2. Which sentence is correct?

Grammar Section 3: Cautious Generalisations

1. Which sentence is appropriately cautious?

2. Complete: “This may help explain why political discussions ___ uncomfortable.”

Thought-Provoking Questions

Discuss these questions in pairs or small groups. Give reasons, examples and possible consequences.

1. Are heuristics more helpful or more dangerous in everyday life?

2. When have you jumped to a conclusion too quickly?

3. Why do challenges to our beliefs sometimes feel personal?

4. Can people become less biased, or only more aware of their biases?

5. How can schools teach students to recognise cognitive bias?

6. Is it always better to slow down before making a decision?

7. How can understanding bias improve political or social discussions?

Challenge: Choose one question and prepare a one-minute answer using cautious language such as: It could be argued that..., One possible concern is..., This becomes more complicated because...

Context

Situation: Two colleagues are discussing a new candidate for a job. One colleague makes assumptions based on a stereotype. The other tries to identify the bias and make the discussion more evidence-based.

Student A

You are making a quick judgement about the candidate. Explain your reasoning, but be open to being challenged.

Student B

You notice a possible cognitive bias. Politely challenge the assumption and ask for evidence.

Role play task: Discuss the candidate for five minutes. By the end, agree on three pieces of evidence you need before making a fair decision.
