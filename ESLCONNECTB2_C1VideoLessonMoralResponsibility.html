Moral Responsibility and Upbringing: B2/C1 Lesson

B2/C1 ESL Connect Video lesson based on the tapescript: JoJo, deep self theory, upbringing, moral competence, compatibilism and responsibility.

Video link: Open on YouTube

Lesson Aim

Students watch a philosophical video about JoJo, a dictator raised in an extreme environment, and discuss whether people are fully morally responsible for actions shaped by upbringing.

Before watching

  1. Can someone be fully responsible for bad actions if they were raised to believe those actions were normal?
  2. What matters more when judging responsibility: the action itself, the person’s values, or how those values were formed?
  3. Should society blame, punish or try to re-educate someone who lacks moral understanding?

B2/C1 Grammar: Hypotheticals, Conditionals and Moral Judgement

This video discusses imagined cases and moral responsibility, so the grammar focus is on advanced conditional structures, concession and evaluative language.

Example: Had JoJo been given a meaningful opportunity to learn right from wrong, we might judge him more harshly.

Grammar Section 1: Third Conditional and Mixed Conditionals

1. If JoJo ___ a normal childhood, he might have developed different values.

2. Had JoJo been taught justice, he ___ less morally incompetent.

3. Which sentence is correct?

Grammar Section 2: Concession and Nuance

1. Which sentence is most nuanced?

2. Complete: “Even if JoJo acts on his own values, ___.”

3. Which sentence uses “whereas” correctly?

Grammar Section 3: Evaluative Language

1. Which phrase is useful for careful moral judgement?

2. Complete: “The more opportunities JoJo had to learn, ___ excusable his actions become.”

3. Which sentence sounds most academic?

Thought-Provoking Questions

Discuss these questions in pairs or small groups. Give reasons, examples and possible consequences.

1. Can someone be fully responsible for actions if their values were shaped by an extreme upbringing?

2. Should we judge people by their actions, their intentions or how their character was formed?

3. If someone lacks moral competence, should they be punished, educated or protected from power?

4. Does a bad childhood reduce blame, or does it only help explain behaviour?

5. What would make JoJo more responsible for his actions?

6. Can people transcend their upbringing and learn right from wrong?

7. How should society balance compassion for someone’s background with justice for their victims?

Challenge: Choose one question and prepare a one-minute answer using cautious language such as: It could be argued that..., One possible concern is..., This becomes more complicated because...

Context

Situation: Two philosophy students are preparing for a seminar. Student A thinks JoJo is fully responsible because he acts according to his own values. Student B thinks his upbringing and lack of moral competence reduce his responsibility.

Student A

Defend the Deep Self View. Argue that JoJo’s actions reflect his true values and commitments.

Student B

Defend Susan Wolf’s view. Argue that JoJo’s values were shaped in a way that reduces his blameworthiness.

Role play task: Discuss the case for five minutes. By the end, agree on one factor that increases responsibility and one factor that reduces it.
