Video link: Open on YouTube

Lesson Aim

Students watch a short video about successful pitching and learn how to present an idea as a compelling story that makes the audience want to say yes.

Video structure: Part 1 explains FOMO, audience motivation and knowing your audience. Part 2 explains the hero’s journey, handling weaknesses and inspiring confidence.

Before watching

  1. Have you ever had to convince someone to support an idea?
  2. What makes a pitch persuasive: facts, emotion, storytelling or confidence?
  3. Why might an audience reject a good idea?

B2/C1 Grammar: Advanced Persuasive Pitching Language

This video is about pitching ideas persuasively, so the grammar focus is on advanced structures used to create urgency, anticipate objections and present consequences clearly.

Example: Were we to ignore this opportunity, we would risk missing out on a project with significant long-term impact.

Thought-Provoking Questions

Discuss these questions in pairs or small groups. Give reasons, examples and possible consequences.

1. Is a good pitch more about facts, emotion or storytelling?

2. Can creating FOMO be persuasive without becoming manipulative?

3. Should a speaker openly discuss the weakest point in their proposal?

4. What makes an audience feel that an idea is too important to ignore?

5. How can a pitch make the audience feel like the hero of the story?

6. Is it better to start a pitch with the problem, the opportunity or the final vision?

7. What ethical limits should there be when trying to persuade people?

Challenge: Choose one question and prepare a one-minute answer using cautious language such as: It could be argued that..., One possible concern is..., This becomes more complicated because...

Context

Situation: Student A is pitching a new workplace project. Student B is a manager who needs to decide whether to support it. The pitch must tell a story, connect to the manager’s goals and address one weakness directly.

Student A: Pitcher

Pitch your idea. Explain the status quo, the problem, your solution and why the manager might miss out if they say no.

Student B: Manager

Listen critically. Ask about the audience, the impact and the weakest point in the idea.

Role play task: Act out the pitch for four minutes. By the end, Student B decides whether to support the idea and explains why.
